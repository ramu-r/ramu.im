## How it grew

The initial system was simple. Daily journal entries. Weekly review. A handful of loops I was tracking.

Then I started adding things.

Health states for each loop — not just active but a progression from Unknown through Struggling, Moving, Consolidating, Stable. Intervention ladders when a loop stagnated. Angle rotation — changing the lens of a question when the same angle kept getting the same non-answer. Time-accounting: working out how much discretionary time I actually had versus how much I was claiming I didn't. Three separate daily skills: one conversational, one for questions and scheduling, one for classification and loop updates. Each phase spec'd out in its own document.

By phase 1.7, using the system properly meant running an hour of work before I could call a day done.

I did it a few times a month.

## The real problem

I told myself the gaps were avoidance — that I knew I should be using it and was finding reasons not to. That framing produced more machinery. More nudges, more failure modes to detect, more automatic escalation.

The real diagnosis came from a different question: what was the time actually buying me?

Almost everything the system produced was input for more machinery. Classifications fed health states. Health states fed frequency tables. Frequency tables fed question scheduling. The user-facing output — findings, strategy recall, direction — was supposed to emerge from all that bookkeeping, but it rarely did. The flagship finding most weeks restated what I already knew.

High cost, no felt benefit. Gaps. No data. No benefit. The spiral was about cost, not avoidance.

## What I cut

The daily skills are gone. There's no required daily skill to run. On days I want to write, I write. On days I want to talk through the day, I talk. The system captures from either. A two-line entry counts.

The loop count is capped at three active at a time. I had thirteen running concurrently. That's twelve questions a week at minimum, and the answer rate collapsed within a month. The cap is structural: a new loop has to displace something, and that friction is the prioritisation pressure I actually needed.

The weekly review is the only intelligence pass now. It reads the week, writes a brief, updates loops, pre-loads next week's questions. The questions carry the strategy payload that makes answering them useful — not bare accountability pings, which demonstrably go unanswered.

## What survived

The loop files. The strategy memory. The weekly brief.

Everything else was machinery for its own sake.

The principle for what to cut: does this produce something I read or act on? If it only feeds something else that feeds something else, cut it. A dumber system that runs beats a smarter one that doesn't.
