I joined Bluesky with some optimism. The pitch was compelling — a decentralized, open-protocol alternative to Twitter, built by people who understood what made early social media work. I gave it a real try.

It didn't stick.

## The promise

The AT Protocol underneath Bluesky is genuinely interesting. Federation, user-owned data, the ability to move your identity across servers — these are ideas worth building on. On paper, Bluesky solves a lot of what made Twitter's late era so frustrating.

## Where it fell flat

**The network wasn't there.** This is the oldest problem with any social platform, and Bluesky hasn't cracked it. The people I follow and the conversations I care about simply weren't happening there. A platform is only as valuable as the people on it, and critical mass matters more than architecture.

**The feeds felt hollow.** Even with custom algorithmic feeds (one of Bluesky's genuinely clever features), I kept finding myself scrolling without landing on anything I wanted to engage with. Volume without signal.

**It felt like Twitter in 2012.** Which is either a feature or a bug depending on who you ask. For me, in 2026, it felt like going backwards — the same interaction patterns, the same dynamics, just with fewer people.

## What would change my mind

If the people I care about showed up, I'd come back. That's really it. The product is fine. The protocol is clever. But the experience is empty without the network, and I'm not willing to wait around for it to fill up again.

Maybe that makes me part of the problem. Maybe everyone sitting it out is the reason it stays thin. I've made peace with that.

For now, I'm writing here instead.

I'm not sure if it's because I'm yet to come across groups and people that make bluesky great. I'm hoping that's the case and I've got my fingers crossed. It was Dan Abramov's article and it's related HN discussion that made me wnat to try this, and I really hope bluesky works out.

Because I've always been a reddit guy and I've always hated the twitter and instagram kinda social meida. Instagram's focus on sharing pictures and videos quickly degrades into engagement bait, and twitter's limits on 120 characters eventually forces one to farm rage bait for attention. With the wolrd moving on to algorithmic feeds, these trends only got worse. Normal text based social media like forums or reddit has been getting more and more extinct and I've been feeling pretty alienated on the current social media landscape.

It didn't help that reddit made things worse and actiely made me feel that social media does not work. My only solace has been places like hacker news or smaller groups on telegram where thoughtful discussions happen. But even hacker news easily veers into long winded debates about tangential discussions sometimes, and telegram really isn't made for the kind of well thought out answers I used to find valuable about reddit.

I had tried both scuttlebutt and nostr before and found both of them lacking. Scuttlebutt didn't allow deleting older posts and I found the thought of filling my phone with social media slop horrifying. Nostr was too too focused on bitcoin to the degree that it pushed out any other discussion that could happen. I wasn't too excited about mastodon or activitypub because it required me to run my own servers, and running my own services quickly devovled into a full time sysadmin job. This was a while ago and I had somewhat lost hope on social media. I never tried bluesky before because I had heard it was run by a VC funded company and it was centralized.

It was on this background that, one day the govt of India banned telegram. That made me pretty conscious about how precarious my social media was, and it was on teh back on my mind that I should find something that would be a bit more resistant to govt censure. In those few days, dan had written that article on bluesky, and it was piqued my curiosity to figure out how bluesky managed its identities if there were instances.

After a bit of google searching, I understood bluesky is basically a distributed version of all the things traditional social media rolled into one monolith. Our identities, the data we post, and the network we connect to, and what shows up in our recommendations, all of these could be setup as differnet pieces, mixed and matched as we find fit.

The things I found appealing about bluesky were
- I could maintain my identity under my own domain. All I had to do was modify some TX records, or host a file
- My data could still be stored in bluesky maintained PLC servers which I could mvoe anytime I want to my own self hosted instance should I feel like it
- I could replace the feed with any alogrithm I want. This was exciting because this was the reason why I like RSS so much, it puts the control on my hand.
- The UI was nice and it seemed like the community was interested in the kind of discussions that pick my mind.

As much as the idea was exciting, the reality didn't live up to what I had expected.

First, bluesky does not support domain based dids, and I'm forced to use one on bluesky's plc. Yes I can sign it with my domain, but its still a plc did and not a did:web one
- as much as different parts of the protocol could be mixed and match, many parts are still maintained by bluesky without alternatives. I could be wrong but I didn't find an easy to run alternative for the appview.
- The discussion didn't have the toxicity I had come across twitter or reddit, but it still felt like I missed something. Maybe I'm missing the early days of the internet where I could come across something I find new and interesting everyday. Maybe it's because I'm saturated that I find nothing interesting. I still remember early reddit, or the earlier days of the internet. Partly it could be due to all the default posts that gets pushed to my feed since I'm followin nobody. I'm hoping this state improves with some careful curation.

But it wasn't all too bad, there were some good parts as well
The custom feeds and the starter packs were good. It was relieving that I could always switch to a different section or view of the ecosystem and I'm not stuck on what I find on there initially
Unlike the rest of current social media ecosystem, like Youtube or Facebook, Bluesky lets me mark what I don't find interesting and I'm hoping in a few days, the algorithm will show me less of americal politics
